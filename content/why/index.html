Why

Why the quicksort algorithm is important, and interesting

Sorting things seems to be a simple task. Everyone can sort a reasonably sized list of numbers - let’s say 10 - in a pretty short period of time. If someone gave you ten playing cards and asked you to sort them from lowest to highest, you could probably do it in less than 10 seconds without a second thought.

While you probably never consciously think about a "strategy" for sorting, your strategy for sorting a hand of cards probably goes something like this. Let's assume you're a patient player and you wait until all your cards are dealt before picking up your hand, and let's also ignore the cards' suits for now. When you pick up the cards, you look at the card furthest to your left. If it’s a high card you might move it to the right, looking for the next-highest card to put it to the right of. If it’s a low card you probably leave it in its current position, and then look at the next card to the right. If that card is lower, you move it to the left in front of the first card you looked at, and so on. Before long, a few seconds, you've sorted your cards from lowest to highest without too much trouble.

Obviously, this sorting method (the Insertion Sort), or slight variations thereof, has worked perfectly well for card games for hundreds of years.

But - what if you were dealt a thousand cards? Or a million? Would this still be the best strategy?

The answer is “usually not.”

There is a sorting method out there that usually sorts large sets of numbers faster than intuitive methods such as the Insertion Sort we use to sort playing cards. So, this sorting algorithm is important to making computing more efficient.

This method is called the quicksort algorithm.
